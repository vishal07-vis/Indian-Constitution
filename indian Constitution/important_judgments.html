Landmark Judgments in Indian Legal History

Background • Issue • Judgment • Impact — exam-friendly layout with search, copy & download.

Kesavananda Bharati vs. State of Kerala (1973)

Background: Kesavananda, head of a Kerala matha, challenged state land reform laws.

Issue: Whether Parliament can amend any part of the Constitution — including Fundamental Rights.

Judgment: Parliament may amend the Constitution but cannot destroy or alter its Basic Structure (majority view).

Impact: Created the Basic Structure Doctrine — protected democracy, secularism, judicial review and key constitutional features from arbitrary amendment.

Golaknath vs. State of Punjab (1967)

Background: Land acquisition and compensation disputes in Punjab; family argued Fundamental Rights infringement.

Judgment: Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights under Article 368 (majority hold).

Impact: Temporarily strengthened FRs; later led to the 24th Amendment (1971) and prepared the path to Kesavananda.

Indira Nehru Gandhi vs. Raj Narain (1975)

Background: Raj Narain challenged Indira Gandhi's election to Lok Sabha alleging corrupt practices.

Judgment: Court invalidated Indira Gandhi's election on grounds of electoral malpractice.

Impact: Reinforced that rule of law applies to all, including the PM; this judgment contributed to events leading to the Emergency (1975–77).

ADM Jabalpur vs. Shivkant Shukla (1976)

Background: During Emergency, petitioners challenged suspension of habeas corpus reliefs.

Judgment: Court held rights to approach under habeas corpus were suspendable during Emergency — citizens could not file habeas corpus.

Impact: Widely condemned as a judicial low point; subsequently effectively overruled by later judgments (e.g., Maneka Gandhi).

Maneka Gandhi vs. Union of India (1978)

Background: Maneka Gandhi's passport was impounded without giving fair hearing.

Judgment: Expanded Article 21: right to life & personal liberty includes fair procedure, dignity, and a broad range of rights — due process was read into Constitution.

Impact: Vastly broadened fundamental rights protection; limited arbitrary executive action and rehabilitated rule of law post-Emergency.

Minerva Mills vs. Union of India (1980)

Background: Challenge to parts of the 42nd Amendment which sought to curtail judicial review and alter FR–DPSP balance.

Judgment: Struck down clauses that breached the basic structure by giving unfettered amending power to Parliament.

Impact: Reinforced Basic Structure doctrine; re-balanced Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles (DPSPs).

SR Bommai vs. Union of India (1994)

Background: Several States dismissed under Article 356; questions on misuse of President's Rule.

Judgment: Presidential proclamations under Art. 356 are subject to judicial review; secularism is part of the Basic Structure.

Impact: Strengthened federalism and curtailed arbitrary dismissals of state governments; affirmed secularism as core constitutional value.

Vishaka vs. State of Rajasthan (1997)

Background: Bhanwari Devi's gang-rape for stopping child marriage raised the issue of sexual harassment at workplace.

Judgment: SC framed Vishaka Guidelines to prevent sexual harassment at workplace until Parliament enacted a law.

Impact: Led to the Protection of Women from Sexual Harassment (POSH) Act, 2013; landmark for women's workplace rights.

Shah Bano Case (Mohammad Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum) (1985)

Background: Shah Bano, a divorced Muslim woman, sought maintenance under CrPC §125.

Judgment: SC granted maintenance, upholding secular application of law to secure justice for a divorced woman.

Impact: Sparked national debate on Uniform Civil Code and personal laws; led to Parliament passing the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986.

Olga Tellis vs. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)

Background: Pavement dwellers challenged eviction orders by BMC.

Judgment: Recognized Right to Livelihood as part of Right to Life (Article 21); evictions required due process and alternative arrangements.

Impact: Strengthened protection for the urban poor; emphasized substance of Article 21 beyond mere existence.

Navtej Singh Johar vs. Union of India (2018)

Background: Challenge to Section 377 IPC criminalising consensual homosexual acts.

Judgment: SC decriminalised consensual homosexual conduct between adults; held that criminalisation violated Right to Equality and Dignity.

Impact: Landmark for LGBTQ rights and equality jurisprudence in India.

Shayara Bano vs. Union of India (2017) — Triple Talaq

Background: Shayara Bano challenged instant triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat).

Judgment: Instant Triple Talaq held unconstitutional and violative of Muslim women's fundamental rights.

Impact: Led to legislative action — Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019; major step for gender justice.

Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs. Union of India (2017)

Background: Challenges around Aadhaar and state surveillance raised the question of privacy.

Judgment: Declared Right to Privacy as intrinsic to Article 21 (Right to Life & Personal Liberty).

Impact: Critical for data protection, biometric ID debates, and personal liberty in digital era.

I.R. Coelho vs. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)

Background: Numerous laws were placed in the 9th Schedule to protect them from judicial scrutiny.

Judgment: Held that laws placed in the 9th Schedule after the inception of Basic Structure doctrine are subject to judicial review if they violate basic structure.

Impact: Prevented misuse of the 9th Schedule as a shield; reaffirmed supremacy of basic constitutional features.

Ayodhya Judgment — M Siddiq vs. Mahant Suresh Das (2019)

Background: Longstanding land dispute over Babri Masjid–Ram Janmabhoomi site between Hindu and Muslim claimants.

Judgment: SC awarded the disputed land to Ram Lalla (a Hindu deity) and directed allocation of alternative land to the Muslim parties.

Impact: Ended a century-old dispute; facilitated steps toward constructing Ram Mandir and sought to balance community interests.
