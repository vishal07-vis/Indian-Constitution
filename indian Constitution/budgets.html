Budget in India — Complete Guide

Articles, types, stages, funds, terms, reforms, and quick revision — with interactive features for study and print.

1. Constitutional Provisions (Articles)

Below are concise explanations of key articles related to budget and finance — each in 4–5 lines for quick memorization.

Article 110 — Money Bill

A Money Bill concerns only taxation, borrowing, payments from the Consolidated Fund, or receipts. It can be introduced only in the Lok Sabha on the President's recommendation. The Speaker certifies a Money Bill and the Rajya Sabha can only recommend changes; it must return to Lok Sabha within 14 days. The Speaker's certificate on Money Bill is final and cannot be questioned in court.

Article 111 — Assent to Bills

After both Houses pass a bill, it is presented to the President who may give assent, withhold assent, or return it (except Money Bills). When returned (non-Money Bill), Parliament may reconsider and pass it again; President may still withhold assent. For Money Bills, President must give assent and cannot return the bill.

Article 112 — Annual Financial Statement

The Annual Financial Statement is the Union Budget — a detailed estimate of receipts and expenditure for the coming financial year. It is divided into Revenue and Capital budgets and presented in Parliament annually by the Finance Minister. It forms the basis for demands for grants and appropriation.

Article 113 — Demands for Grants

Demands for Grants are the detailed estimates of expenditure submitted to Lok Sabha department-wise. Only Lok Sabha has the power to vote and pass these demands; Rajya Sabha may discuss but cannot vote. Voting on demands is essential before money is withdrawn from the Consolidated Fund for specific heads.

Article 114 — Appropriation Bill

The Appropriation Bill authorizes the government to withdraw funds from the Consolidated Fund for the purposes and amounts approved by Parliament. It follows the passing of Demands for Grants; no money can be withdrawn without an Appropriation Act. It usually accompanies the Finance Bill during budget closure.

Article 115 — Supplementary, Additional and Excess Grants

If expenditures exceed the sanctioned budget, or new services arise, the government seeks Supplementary, Additional or Excess Grants. Supplementary Grants are for additional requirements; Additional Grants are for new services; Excess Grants are for expenditures unauthorized earlier and require retrospective approval.

Article 116 — Vote on Account, Vote of Credit, Exceptional Grants

Vote on Account allows temporary withdrawal of funds pending full budget approval (commonly used during elections). Vote of Credit is for unforeseen urgent expenditure (e.g., war), and Exceptional Grants refer to special non-recurring needs; Parliament authorizes these as required to maintain government functioning.

Article 117 — Special Provisions for Financial Bills

Financial Bills include both money and non-money provisions and need President's recommendation before introduction. Unlike pure Money Bills, Rajya Sabha has greater participation and may amend financial bills, though Money Bill rules do not apply. The article ensures special procedures for mixed-content bills.

Article 148 — Comptroller and Auditor-General (CAG)

CAG audits Union and State government accounts, public sector undertakings, and reports on public finances. Appointed by the President, CAG holds office for six years or until age 65 and functions independently to ensure fiscal accountability. CAG's audit reports are vital for legislative scrutiny and appear before Parliament and state legislatures.

Articles 149–151 — Duties & Reports of CAG

These articles define CAG's powers (audit scope, audit of companies), prescribe the form and maintenance of government accounts, and require that CAG's reports be submitted to the President/Governor and laid before legislatures. They institutionalize the audit process and reporting cycle.

Article 265 — Taxes

"No tax shall be levied or collected except by authority of law." This fundamental limit ensures taxation requires legislative backing, protecting citizens from arbitrary taxation and ensuring parliamentary control over tax laws.

Article 266 — Consolidated Fund, Contingency Fund, and Public Account

Article 266 organizes the three principal government accounts: Consolidated Fund (all revenues and public expenditure), Contingency Fund (for urgent unexpected expenses at President's disposal), and Public Account (trust and deposit liabilities held by government). Withdrawals from the Consolidated Fund require parliamentary approval.

Article 267 — Contingency Fund of India

Contingency Fund is established and kept at the President's disposal to meet unforeseen or urgent expenditures. Advances from this fund are later regularized by Parliament through Appropriation Bills. It ensures immediate availability of funds in emergencies.

Article 280 — Finance Commission

Constituted by the President every five years, the Finance Commission recommends distribution of net proceeds of taxes between Union and States, and grants-in-aid to states. It aims to secure fiscal federalism and adjust vertical and horizontal imbalances in resource allocation.

Article 293 — Borrowing by States

States can borrow within India, but if they have outstanding loans from the Union, further borrowing may need the Union's consent. The article regulates state-level borrowing to maintain macro-fiscal stability and coordination between Union and states.

Article 360 — Financial Emergency

Allows the President to proclaim a Financial Emergency if India's financial stability is threatened. During such a proclamation, the Centre can direct states to observe certain financial measures and reduce salaries of Govt. officials; it enhances Union control over finances to restore stability.

2. Types of Budgets
  • Union Budget: Annual estimate for the Union; presented by Finance Minister.
  • State Budget: Annual budget of each state managed by its finance minister.
  • Interim Budget: Temporary cover for months till new govt presents full budget (commonly before elections).
  • Vote-on-Account: Short-term permission to withdraw funds till full budget passes.
  • Performance Budget: Links allocation to outputs/outcomes of schemes.
  • Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB): All expenditures justified anew rather than adjusted from previous year.
  • Outcome Budget: Focuses on performance indicators and results (introduced 2005–06).
  • Gender Budgeting: Allocations targeting women's welfare and gender equality.
  • Green Budget: Environmental and sustainability-focused allocations.

3. Structure of the Union Budget

Revenue Budget

Revenue Receipts include tax revenue (income tax, GST, customs) and non-tax revenue (dividends, fees, interest). Revenue Expenditure covers routine expenses: subsidies, salaries, interest payments and pensions — these do not create assets but are recurring in nature.

Capital Budget

Capital Receipts are borrowings, recoveries of loans, and other capital receipts. Capital Expenditure is on long-term assets such as infrastructure, defence capital works and investments that create assets or reduce liabilities.

4. Classification of Budgets & Deficits

Balanced Budget: Receipts = Expenditure. Surplus Budget: Receipts > Expenditure. Deficit Budget: Expenditure > Receipts (most common).

Fiscal Deficit: Total expenditure minus total receipts (excluding borrowings). It indicates government’s borrowing requirement. Revenue Deficit: Revenue Expenditure minus Revenue Receipts. Primary Deficit: Fiscal deficit minus interest payments.

Effective Revenue Deficit: Revenue deficit less grants for capital expenditure — shows mismatch in revenue account that affects investment.

5. Stages of Budget Making (Detailed)

Preparation (Sept–Jan)

Finance Ministry circulates guidelines; ministries submit demand estimates; consultations with RBI, NITI Aayog, CAG inputs; iterative meetings with PM and Cabinet. The Budget Call is issued, setting timelines for estimates.

Presentation (1st Feb)

Finance Minister presents Budget in the Lok Sabha at 11:00 AM (since 2017). Budget speech has Part A (economic review & broad policies) and Part B (tax proposals). Accompanied by documents: Estimates, Finance Bill, Appropriation Bill, Expenditure Budget, Receipts Budget, etc.

Parliamentary Discussion

General discussion (no voting), then Demands for Grants examined by Estimates Committee/Standing Committees. Lok Sabha votes on Demands for Grants. Cut motions can be moved — Policy Cut (symbolic protest), Economy Cut (reduce to specific amount), Token Cut (small sum).

Passing of Bills

After voting, Appropriation Bill authorizes withdrawal; Finance Bill enacts taxation measures. Money Bills follow special procedure (Art. 110) where Rajya Sabha's role is limited.

Implementation & Audit

Funds are released to ministries; expenditure recorded in Consolidated Fund accounts; CAG audits performance and compliance, and presents reports to the legislature for scrutiny.

6. Funds of India (Explained)

Consolidated Fund of India (CFI)

All government revenues, loans raised, and receipts are credited here. Withdrawals require parliamentary authorization through Appropriation Acts. It's the primary account governing all public expenditure.

Contingency Fund of India

Set up for urgent unforeseen needs; initially used as advances by President and later regularized by Parliament's approval. Ensures immediate funds during crises without waiting for parliamentary sanction.

Public Account

Contains funds held in trust by the government (e.g., provident funds, small savings) that are repayable and do not require parliamentary appropriation to be disbursed.

7. Important Budgetary Terms
  • Fiscal Deficit: Indicates borrowings required by the government to meet its total expenditure beyond receipts.
  • Revenue Deficit: Shortfall in current account; funds required to meet recurring expenses.
  • Primary Deficit: Fiscal deficit excluding interest payments; shows current-year borrowing requirement for new expenditure.
  • Effective Revenue Deficit: Revenue deficit excluding grants for capital assets, focuses on revenue account imbalance.

8. Reforms & Amendments
  • 101st Amendment (2016): Introduced Goods and Services Tax (GST) — major tax reform unifying indirect taxes.
  • 2017 Reforms: Moved budget date to 1st Feb and merged Railway Budget with Union Budget for integrated fiscal planning.
  • 1991 Reforms: Liberalisation and fiscal consolidation following the balance of payments crisis — emphasis on reducing deficits and market reforms.

9. Committees & Oversight

Key legislative oversight bodies include the Public Accounts Committee (examines CAG reports), the Estimates Committee (examines estimates and policy), and the Committee on Public Undertakings (examines PSUs). These committees ensure accountability and suggest improvements.

10. Special Features & Traditions
  • Halwa Ceremony: Traditional distribution of halwa to those involved with budget printing.
  • Budget Secrecy: Strict procedures to prevent leaks until formal presentation.
  • Economic Survey: Released a day before the Budget, reviewing the economy and informing policy choices.

Quick Summary

Budget (Article 112) is the Annual Financial Statement, structured into Revenue and Capital budgets, passed through a parliamentary process involving Demands for Grants, Appropriation Bill and Finance Bill, and audited by the CAG. Fiscal indicators like fiscal deficit and revenue deficit guide fiscal policy; reforms like GST and merger of Railway Budget are landmark developments.
