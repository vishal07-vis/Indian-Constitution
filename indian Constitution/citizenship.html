Citizenship in India

Complete notes: Constitution, Citizenship Act, Amendments, OCI/PIO, acquisition & loss, debates.

1. Meaning of Citizenship

click to toggle

Citizenship is the legal recognition of belonging to a country. Citizens have full political & civil rights (vote, contest elections, hold public office). Non-citizens/aliens have limited rights (e.g., right to life).

India has Single Citizenship — national citizenship only (no state citizenship).

2. Citizenship in the Constitution (Articles 5–11)

click to toggle

Article 5 – Citizenship at commencement

Person domiciled in India became citizen if: born in India; or either parent born in India; or ordinarily resident for 5 years before 26 Jan 1950.

Article 6 – Migration from Pakistan

Those who migrated to India before 19 July 1948 became citizens automatically; those after had to register and reside 6 months.

Article 7 – Migration to Pakistan

Those who migrated to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 lost Indian citizenship; could regain if returned under resettlement permit.

Article 8 – Indians living abroad

Persons of Indian origin abroad could register at Indian posts and become citizens.

Article 9 – Foreign citizenship

Voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship caused loss of Indian citizenship.

Article 10 & 11

Article 10: Continuance subject to law. Article 11: Parliament empowered to make laws on citizenship → Citizenship Act, 1955.

3. Citizenship Act, 1955 (Acquisition & Loss)

click to toggle

The Act (with later amendments) sets out methods of acquiring and losing citizenship after 26 Jan 1950.

Acquisition of Citizenship

  • By Birth:
    • Born 1950–1 July 1987 → citizen by birth.
    • Born 1 July 1987–3 Dec 2004 → at least one parent Indian.
    • Born after 3 Dec 2004 → both parents Indian OR one parent Indian & other NOT an illegal migrant.
  • By Descent:
    • Born outside India 1950–10 Dec 1992 → father Indian.
    • After 10 Dec 1992 → either parent Indian.
    • After 2004 → registration of birth at Indian consulate within 1 year.
  • By Registration: Persons of Indian origin, spouses of Indian citizens, children can register after qualifying residence (usually 7 years).
  • By Naturalisation: Foreigners may naturalise after ~12 years residence (11+1) subject to good character and renouncing prior nationality.
  • By Incorporation of Territory: If territory becomes part of India, its residents become Indian citizens (e.g., Sikkim, Pondicherry).

Loss of Citizenship

  • Renunciation: Voluntary surrender.
  • Termination: Acquiring another country's citizenship voluntarily.
  • Deprivation: Cancelled by government for fraud, disloyalty, unlawful acts (subject to law).

4. Major Amendments to Citizenship Law

click to toggle
  • 1986: Birth right tightened — at least one parent must be Indian.
  • 1992: Descent through mother included (gender-equality step).
  • 2003: Concept of illegal migrant introduced; NRC idea; introduced OCI category.
  • 2005 & 2015: Expanded OCI concessions.
  • 2019 (CAA): Fast-tracked citizenship for persecuted non-Muslim minorities (Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, Christians) from Pakistan, Afghanistan, Bangladesh who entered before 31 Dec 2014; reduced residency to 5 years; excluded Muslims (controversial).
Note: Amendments and laws are subject to judicial review and political debate.

5. Overseas Citizenship of India (OCI)

click to toggle

Introduced in 2005. OCI is a long-term visa and identification for persons of Indian origin (excluding Pakistan & Bangladesh nationals in practice). Key points:

  • Multiple-entry, lifelong visa to India.
  • Right to live, work and study in India.
  • No political rights — cannot vote or hold constitutional offices.
  • Cannot purchase agricultural land.

PIO scheme merged into OCI (2015).

6. Person of Indian Origin (PIO)

click to toggle

PIO cards existed earlier to help diaspora; merged with OCI in 2015. Now OCI is the primary route for overseas Indians for long-term association with India without full citizenship.

7. Important Features of Indian Citizenship

click to toggle
  • Single Citizenship for the whole Republic.
  • No Dual Citizenship — holding another nationality means loss/termination of Indian citizenship.
  • Union Control: Citizenship is regulated by Parliament (Union list power).
  • Certain FRs reserved for citizens: Articles 15, 16, 19, 29, 30 grant special privileges to citizens (e.g., vote, contest, govt jobs).

8. Citizenship vs Nationality

click to toggle

Nationality is an international law concept denoting membership of a sovereign state. Citizenship denotes the domestic legal rights and duties. Every citizen is a national; some nationals (e.g., OCI) are not full citizens.

9. Citizenship Debate in India

click to toggle

Key contemporary issues:

  • NRC in Assam: Process to identify citizens caused displacement concerns, documentation debates.
  • CAA 2019: Controversy over excluding Muslims, humanitarians vs security debate.
  • Balancing refugee protection, minority persecution, sovereignty and security remains contested.

Summary

click to toggle
  1. Articles 5–11 defined citizenship at commencement; Parliament empowered to legislate (Citizenship Act, 1955).
  2. Acquisition: Birth, Descent, Registration, Naturalisation, Incorporation of territory.
  3. Loss: Renunciation, Termination, Deprivation.
  4. OCI/PIO provide diaspora ties without political rights.
  5. Major amendments: 1986, 1992, 2003, 2019 (CAA).
  6. Unique features: Single citizenship, no dual citizenship, Union control.
