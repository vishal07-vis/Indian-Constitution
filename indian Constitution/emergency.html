Emergency Provisions (Articles 352–360)

Part XVIII — Compact revision guide with expandable articles, table and quick exam tips.

Article 352 – National Emergency

Grounds: War, External Aggression, Armed Rebellion (44th Amendment).

Process: President proclaims on written advice of Union Cabinet. Approval required within 1 month by Parliament. Duration: 6 months at a time (extendable).

Effects: Centre gains power to legislate on State List; Article 19 suspended automatically; other FRs can be restricted (Art. 359 orders) except Art. 20 & 21; tenure of legislatures can be extended by 1 year at a time while Emergency lasts + 6 months after it ends.

Usage: Declared in 1962, 1971, 1975.

Article 353 – Effect of Proclamation of Emergency

Union executive and Parliament powers extend during National Emergency.

While a National Emergency is in force, the executive power of the Union extends to giving directions to any State. Parliament gains power to legislate on State List subjects.

Article 354 – Application of provisions relating to distribution of revenues

President can modify Centre–State financial relations in Emergency.

During National Emergency the President may make changes to how revenues are distributed between the Centre and States, temporarily overriding normal Finance Commission recommendations.

Article 355 – Duty of the Union to protect States

Union's duty to protect States & ensure constitutional governance.

The Union has a constitutional duty to protect states against external aggression and internal disturbance and to ensure that state governance conforms to the Constitution. This article underpins the use of Article 356 in practice.

Article 356 – State Emergency / President’s Rule

Failure of constitutional machinery in a State; Governor's report or President's satisfaction.

Process: President issues proclamation; must be approved by Parliament within 2 months; valid for 6 months and extendable (subject to limits: max 3 years with conditions beyond 1 year).

Effects: President assumes State functions; Assembly may be dissolved or suspended; Parliament legislates for State; expenditures charged to Consolidated Fund of State may be authorized by President.

Controversy: Article 356 has been widely criticised for misuse – it has been invoked over 100 times in independent India.

Article 357 – Exercise of legislative powers under Proclamation issued under Article 356

Parliament/President may legislate for the State when President's Rule is in force.

During President's Rule, Parliament can delegate legislative power to the President or to the Governor of the State to make laws for the State. Laws made during President's Rule continue to remain valid even after normalcy is restored unless altered.

Article 358 – Suspension of provisions of Article 19 during Emergency

Article 19 suspended during National Emergency (war/external aggression).

When a National Emergency is proclaimed on grounds of war or external aggression, the rights under Article 19 (freedom of speech, movement, etc.) are automatically suspended for the duration of the Emergency. Following the 44th Amendment, Article 19 is not suspended if Emergency is declared solely on ground of "armed rebellion".

Article 359 – Suspension of enforcement of Fundamental Rights during Emergency

President may suspend right to move courts for enforcement of certain FRs (except Arts. 20 & 21).

The President may pass an order under Article 359 suspending the right to move any court for enforcement of certain Fundamental Rights (except Articles 20 and 21) while Emergency is in force. This allows Parliament to enact laws which would otherwise infringe these rights.

Article 360 – Financial Emergency

Declared when financial stability or credit of India is threatened.

Process: President proclaims Financial Emergency; must be approved by Parliament within 2 months. No fixed maximum duration — continues until revoked.

Effects: President can direct States on financial measures; can reduce salaries of government employees including judges; may require Presidential assent for State Money Bills. Note: No Financial Emergency has been declared in India so far.

Exam tips
  • Remember: National Emergency used 3 times; Financial Emergency never.
  • 44th Amendment (1978) – "internal disturbance" → "armed rebellion"; Art.20 & 21 protected.
  • Article 356 — most misused; quote cases like S.R. Bommai v. Union of India when making a legal argument (for advanced study).
