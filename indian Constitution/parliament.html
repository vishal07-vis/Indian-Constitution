Parliament of India

Articles, composition, procedures, privileges, bills, amendments, special terms — all organised for study & revision.

1. What is Parliament?

Definition: The Parliament of India is the supreme law-making body at the Union level. It represents the sovereignty of the people. Structure (Article 79): President, Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha. Note: A bill requires President's assent to become law.

3. Composition of Houses

Lok Sabha (Art. 81)

Max strength: 552 — 530 (States) + 20 (UTs) + 2 Anglo-Indian nominees (abolished 104th Amendment, 2019). Current strength: 543. Term: 5 years (can be dissolved).

Rajya Sabha (Art. 80)

Max strength: 250 — 238 elected by state/UT legislatures by proportional representation; 12 nominated by President (people with distinction in literature, art, science & social service). Permanent body; 1/3rd retire every 2 years.

4. Qualifications of MPs (Art. 84)
  • Citizen of India.
  • Minimum age: 25 for Lok Sabha; 30 for Rajya Sabha.
  • Must also satisfy qualifications as per the Representation of People Act, 1951.
5. Disqualifications (Art. 102 + RPA, 1951)
  • Holding an office of profit under the government (exceptions by law).
  • Declared of unsound mind by a competent court.
  • Undischarged insolvent.
  • Not a citizen of India or acquiring foreign citizenship.
  • Disqualified under electoral laws — e.g., corruption, certain criminal convictions, anti-defection rules (10th Schedule).

Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule)

Added by the 52nd Amendment (1985). A member is disqualified if they voluntarily give up party membership or vote/abstain contrary to the party whip. Exceptions exist for mergers (usually 2/3rd).

6. Reservation of Seats (Art. 330–334)

Seats are reserved for Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST) in the Lok Sabha and state assemblies. Anglo-Indian nomination was abolished by the 104th Constitutional Amendment (2019). The 106th Amendment (2023) provides for 33% reservation for women in Lok Sabha and state assemblies; implementation depends on delimitation.

7. Oath & Affirmation (Art. 99)

Every MP must take an oath or affirmation before they enter Parliament — the form is given in the Third Schedule. The oath is administered by the President or a person authorised by the President.

8. Salaries & Allowances (Art. 106)

Salaries and allowances of MPs are determined by law. Typical components include basic salary, daily allowance (for attending sittings), constituency allowance, travel and medical concessions, accommodation and pension after retirement.

9. Parliamentary Privileges (Art. 105)
  • Freedom of Speech in Parliament; no civil or criminal proceedings for things said or votes cast in Parliament.
  • Immunity from arrest in civil cases during the session and 40 days before/after sessions.
  • Exemption from attendance as jurors and witnesses.
  • Right to publish proceedings (with exceptions for secret sittings).

Breach of privilege can lead to punishment (contempt of Parliament).

10. Officers of Parliament

Lok Sabha

  • Speaker (Art. 93) — elected by Lok Sabha, presides over sittings, maintains discipline, certifies Money Bills, decides on disqualification under the Anti-Defection Law. Removal by resolution of majority (Art. 94).
  • Deputy Speaker — acts in absence of Speaker.

Rajya Sabha

  • Chairman — The Vice-President of India is ex-officio Chairman.
  • Deputy Chairman — elected by Rajya Sabha members.
11. Leader of Opposition

Recognised when the largest opposition party has at least 10% of the total seats in the House. The Leader of Opposition gets a status and facilities roughly equivalent to a Cabinet Minister and is a member of certain high-level selection committees.

12. Representation of States & UTs

Allocation of seats in Lok Sabha is based on population. States elect their MPs directly. Union Territories with legislatures (like Delhi and Puducherry) also elect MPs; other UTs have direct representation according to law.

13. Sessions of Parliament (Art. 85)

Parliament meets in three main sessions — Budget, Monsoon and Winter. The Constitution mandates that the gap between two sessions shall not exceed six months. Common features:

  • Question Hour — first hour where MPs ask questions to Ministers.
  • Zero Hour — an Indian parliamentary practice (not in Constitution) after Question Hour for raising urgent matters.
  • Lame Duck Session — last session before dissolution of Lok Sabha; used for pending business.
  • Joint Sitting (Art. 108) — called by President to resolve deadlocks on ordinary bills; presided over by the Speaker of Lok Sabha.
14. Committees of Parliament

Committees are essential for detailed scrutiny. Two broad kinds:

  • Standing Committees — permanent (e.g., Public Accounts Committee, Estimates Committee, Committee on Petitions).
  • Ad-hoc Committees — set up for specific tasks and dissolved after completion.
15. Types of Bills

Ordinary Bill (Art. 107)

Any bill that is not a Money Bill or Constitution Amendment. Can be introduced in either House.

Money Bill (Art. 110)

Deals with matters like taxation, borrowing, Consolidated Fund and Grants. Can be introduced only in Lok Sabha; Speaker's certificate is final. Rajya Sabha can only recommend amendments (Lok Sabha may accept or reject them).

Finance Bill

Introduced along with the Budget; covers taxation and other fiscal measures — may contain provisions that are not strictly Money Bills.

Appropriation Bill

Authorizes withdrawal from the Consolidated Fund of India for specific purposes as approved by Parliament.

Constitution Amendment Bill (Art. 368)

Needs special majority — a majority of total membership and 2/3rd of members present & voting. Some amendments also require ratification by half the states.

Private Member Bill

Introduced by MPs who are not Ministers — rarely passed but useful to highlight issues.

16. Law-Making Procedure
  1. First Reading — Introduction (title & objectives).
  2. Second Reading — General discussion, referral to committee for clause-by-clause examination, and voting on clauses.
  3. Third Reading — Final debate and vote on the bill as a whole.
  4. Sent to the other House to undergo same process.
  5. President's assent — The President may give assent, withhold assent, or return the bill (if not a Money Bill).
17. Important Constitutional Amendments related to Parliament
  • 42nd (1976) — Strengthened centre, expanded Parliament's power, curtailed some judicial review.
  • 44th (1978) — Restored powers taken away by 42nd.
  • 52nd (1985) — Added the Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule).
  • 61st (1989) — Reduced voting age to 18 (for Assembly & Lok Sabha).
  • 91st (2003) — Strengthened anti-defection provisions.
  • 104th (2019) — Abolished Anglo-Indian nomination to Lok Sabha and State Assemblies.
  • 105th (2021) — Gave states power to maintain their OBC lists.
  • 106th (2023) — Provided reservation for women (33%) in Lok Sabha and State Assemblies (pending implementation steps).
18. Special Terms & Extra Concepts
  • Kitchen Cabinet — An informal group of trusted advisers to the Prime Minister, not part of the formal Cabinet.
  • Shadow Cabinet — A feature of parliamentary systems like the UK where the opposition keeps a parallel set of spokespeople; not an official Indian practice but sometimes informally used by opposition parties.

Summary (Quick Revision)

Parliament = President + Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha. It makes laws, controls finances, holds the government accountable and represents the people. Key features: qualifications, disqualifications, privileges, types of bills, law-making procedure, sessions, committees and important amendments.
