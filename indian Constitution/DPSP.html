Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)

Part IV (Articles 36–51) — non-justiciable guidelines for the State to build a welfare republic.

1. Meaning & Nature

DPSPs are policy directions placed in Part IV (Arts. 36–51) telling the State the ends to be achieved — they are fundamental in governance but non-justiciable (courts cannot enforce them).

Purpose: to create a social and economic democracy (welfare state) that complements political democracy protected by Fundamental Rights.

2. Historical Background & Sources

DPSPs are mainly inspired by the Irish Constitution (1937) and the Government of India Act, 1935 (Instrument of Instructions). Influences also include constitutional conventions and welfare ideas from other democracies.

3. Classification (Short)

For study, DPSPs are grouped as Socialist, Gandhian and Liberal‑Intellectual principles; each cluster emphasizes different state goals (welfare, village economy, liberal institutions).

Socialist Principles (Arts 38–43, 47)

Expand
  • Art 38 — State to promote welfare and minimize inequalities.
  • Art 39 — Direct State policy for adequate livelihood, equal pay, prevent wealth concentration, protect children & workers.
  • Art 39A — (42nd Amend.) Free legal aid and equal justice.
  • Art 41 — Right to work, education & public assistance in distress.
  • Art 42 — Humane working conditions & maternity relief.
  • Art 43 — Living wage, cottage industries promotion.
  • Art 47 — Improve nutrition & public health; prohibition of intoxicants (moral/social objective).

Gandhian Principles (Arts 40, 43A, 43B, 46, 47, 48)

Expand
  • Art 40 — Organise village panchayats as self-governing units.
  • Art 43A — (42nd Amend.) Worker participation in management.
  • Art 43B — (97th Amend., 2011) Promotion of co-operative societies.
  • Art 46 — Promote education & economic interests of SC/ST & weaker sections.
  • Art 47 — (also Gandhian) Nutrition and public health duty of State.
  • Art 48 — Organise agriculture and protect animal husbandry; (cow slaughter prohibition element).

Liberal‑Intellectual Principles (Arts 44, 45, 48A, 49, 50, 51)

Expand
  • Art 44 — Uniform Civil Code to promote national integration.
  • Art 45 — Originally free & compulsory education; modified (86th Amend.) to early childhood care (0–6 yrs).
  • Art 48A — (42nd Amend.) Protect and improve environment, forests & wildlife.
  • Art 49 — Protect monuments & heritage.
  • Art 50 — Separate judiciary from executive.
  • Art 51 — Promote international peace, respect for international law.

4. Important Amendments (DPSP)

Read
  1. 42nd Amendment (1976) — Added Art 39A (legal aid), Art 48A (environment), and reinforced socialism & Gandhian elements.
  2. 44th Amendment (1978) — Re-classified Right to Property (Art 300A), strengthening socio‑economic DPSPs.
  3. 86th Amendment (2002) — Modified Art 45 (early childhood care); made education 6–14 a Fundamental Right (Art 21A).
  4. 97th Amendment (2011) — Added Art 43B (promotion of co‑operative societies).

5. DPSPs vs Fundamental Rights

Read

Fundamental Rights are enforceable in courts (individual rights); DPSPs are non‑enforceable guidelines (state policy) aimed at social & economic justice. The Supreme Court (Kesavananda Bharati, 1973) held that a balance between them forms part of the Constitution’s basic structure.

6. Significance

Read

DPSPs act as a blueprint for governance, instructing the State on policy priorities and social goals. They are used by courts as interpretative aids and by governments as targets for law and policy-making.
